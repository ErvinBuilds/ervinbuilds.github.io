// persuasive letter · english 2201

Dear Future Software Engineers,

Antonio Ervin  ·  Mr. Joshua Gardner  ·  21 November 2025

Artificial intelligence (AI) is reshaping the landscape of software engineering in ways that are both daunting and promising. As you prepare to enter this field, you may hear conflicting narratives: some warn of job losses, while others emphasize new opportunities. The truth lies in a nuanced middle ground. AI is not simply a threat — it is a transformative force that requires adaptability, creativity, and resilience. This letter aims to persuade you that despite the challenges, software engineering remains a vital and rewarding career path.

"A study was conducted at Stanford where there was a 13 percent relative decline in employment for early-aged software engineers in AI-exposed jobs."
Sundeep Teki · sundeepteki.org · August 2025
// the challenge

The Disruption Is Real

AI has already begun to automate many tasks traditionally performed by junior engineers. Generative AI excels at codified knowledge — boilerplate coding, standard algorithms, and repetitive tasks — areas where new graduates once gained their footing.

This shift has disrupted the traditional apprenticeship model, leaving fewer opportunities for juniors to build tacit knowledge through hands-on practice. Industry leaders acknowledge this disruption openly.

OpenAI's agentic software engineer (A-SWE) can generate applications, perform bug testing, and write documentation — tasks that once formed the foundation of entry-level work. As a result, companies may hire fewer junior developers, creating anxiety about the profession's future.

// what AI is replacing
Immediate Impact
Boilerplate Coding
Repetitive, formulaic code that junior engineers once wrote to build experience is now auto-generated by tools like GitHub Copilot and ChatGPT.
Near-Term Impact
Standard Algorithms & Bug Fixes
AI agents can now locate and patch common bugs, write unit tests, and implement standard sorting and searching routines faster than a human junior developer.
Emerging Impact
Documentation & DevOps Tasks
Agentic AI systems are moving into planning, execution, and adaptation of tasks originally done by humans — shifting engineers from authoring to overseeing.
"The U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics projects a 17.9% growth in software developer employment between 2023 and 2033 — far above the average for all occupations."
John Daniel · TechPilot · November 2024
// the opportunity

AI Is Augmenting Engineers

Yet, AI is not simply replacing engineers — it is augmenting them. Generative AI tools streamline coding, testing, and documentation, allowing developers to focus on higher-value tasks. The growth projections reflect increasing demand for engineers who can integrate AI into workflows, design complex systems, and ensure reliability.

AI also enhances productivity by automating repetitive tasks, improving bug detection, and supporting DevOps efficiency. Rather than eliminating jobs, AI is creating new roles in machine learning, data science, cybersecurity, and AI architecture.

Google has already reported that 25% of internal code is written by AI — and those engineers aren't out of a job. They're reviewing, orchestrating, and directing AI output toward business goals. This is the new engineering role.

The problem is real: AI is reshaping entry-level opportunities and automating routine tasks. But the solution is equally clear — adaptability, continuous learning, and embracing AI as a partner. Software engineering is not disappearing. It is evolving.

By joining the field, you will not only secure a rewarding career but also play a pivotal role in shaping the future of technology. Future software engineers shouldn't be worried about AI exposure since there are so many opportunities where jobs could be open from AI itself. Key Insights

What six research sources agree on — and what it means for the next generation of software engineers.

// what's changing

The Disruptions

01
Entry-Level Role Compression
A Stanford study found a 13% relative decline in employment for early-career engineers in AI-exposed positions since 2022. Generative AI handles the boilerplate work that once gave juniors their foundation. The apprenticeship model is being disrupted.
02
The Autonomous Agent Shift
Companies are moving from manual completion to autonomous AI agents that can plan, execute, and adapt tasks — including debugging and interacting with compilers. This shifts engineer roles from coding to goal-setting and system oversight.
03
Technical Debt from AI Code
AI agents can drastically reduce coding time, especially for junior developers — but risk adding technical debt if output isn't reviewed. Engineers may spend more time monitoring and correcting AI mistakes than innovating.
04
Tacit Knowledge Gap
Senior engineers benefit most from AI because productivity gains compound on top of tacit knowledge they've already built. Juniors lose the hands-on experience that once built that foundation — creating a growing skills gap.
05
Hiring Practice Disruption
Traditional whiteboard coding interviews no longer accurately measure engineer competence in an AI-assisted world. Companies must evolve assessments to measure how candidates collaborate with AI tools, not just whether they can write algorithms from scratch.
06
Domain Inequality
Not all software engineering roles are affected equally. Frontend and boilerplate-heavy roles face more immediate pressure, while backend systems, AI/ML, cybersecurity, and cloud architecture roles are growing and largely human-dependent.
"Human contextual awareness and human creativity will remain indispensable. Collaboration between AI and developers will drive innovation across industries."
GeeksforGeeks · July 2025
// what's growing

The Opportunities

01
Explosive Job Growth
17.9% projected growth in software developer employment (2023–2033) — far above the national average. AI is expanding the total scope of software engineering rather than replacing it wholesale. The field is getting bigger, not smaller.
02
The AI Salary Premium
Engineers with AI-centric skills earn nearly 18% more than peers without them. As AI tools become standard, the gap between engineers who can use them expertly and those who can't will widen — creating a significant income premium for early adopters.
03
New Career Tracks
AI is creating entirely new roles: ML engineers, AI architects, ethics-compliance leaders, domain specialists, and AI managers. These positions require the human judgment, creativity, and contextual awareness that AI cannot replicate.
04
Exploding AI Software Market
Global AI software is projected to reach $632 billion by 2028 — growing at ~29% annually. This isn't a shrinking industry. The engineers who position themselves at the frontier of this market will have extraordinary career leverage.
05
The Irreplaceable Human Layer
AI cannot replicate complex stakeholder dialogue, ethical judgment, creative problem-solving, or the contextual insight required to align software with business goals. Engineers who cultivate these meta-skills become increasingly valuable as AI handles routine work.
06
Cybersecurity & Cloud Growth
Cybersecurity, blockchain, cloud infrastructure, and IoT/industrial systems are among the fastest-growing engineering domains — all areas where AI augments human capability rather than replacing it, requiring deep domain expertise and human oversight.
// the playbook

What Future Engineers Should Do

Right Now
Learn Prompt Engineering & AI Debugging
The most valuable near-term skill is knowing how to get high-quality output from AI tools and how to critically evaluate what they produce. This is already differentiating engineers in the job market.
Near Term
Specialize in Backend, Cloud, or ML
Backend development, distributed systems, cloud infrastructure, and machine learning are in high demand and largely insulated from AI automation. Depth in these areas is a strong career foundation.
Long Term
Cultivate Meta-Skills
Critical thinking, communication, architectural reasoning, and business acumen — the ability to align software with strategic goals — are skills AI cannot replicate and that grow more valuable as automation increases.

"Those who treat AI as a collaborator rather than a competitor will thrive."

Sundeep Teki · August 2025

The consensus across all six research sources is clear: the question is not whether AI will change software engineering — it's whether you will adapt.

Developers who embrace AI are likely to thrive in the dynamic job market. Past examples show that while some roles decline sharply, others grow or remain stable. The engineers who position themselves on the right side of this shift will define the next era of the profession.
